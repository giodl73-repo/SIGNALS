## Research Question Findings

### RQ1: Does C-38's terminal verdict close a pipeline asymmetry that prose readiness conclusions leave open for external consumers?

**YES -- terminal verdict closes the sole asymmetric node in the full named-binary chain.**

V-01 isolates the contribution precisely. With C-38 PASS, STEP E is no longer the only step that consumes named inputs without producing a named output. The 12th ARCHITECTURE row (`Terminal readiness: PROCEED/LOOP/INVESTIGATE | PART 2 STEP E | topic namespace`) makes the signal-check result accessible as a named artifact to downstream consumers (e.g., the topic namespace's readiness registry) without requiring them to parse STEP E's advisory prose to infer the verdict.

The asymmetry that C-38 closes is structural, not rhetorical: prose "Overall read: proceed / loop once more / investigate further" is semantically equivalent to the terminal verdict but is inaccessible as a named artifact. A topic namespace consuming the signal-check output would need to re-derive the verdict from prose -- the same re-derivation problem that C-19 closed for SEQUENCE and C-22 closed for the inertia section. C-38 applies the same structural closure at the register boundary.

### RQ2: Does C-37's step-granular Consumed-by reduce audit burden from section-traversal to table-scan for PART 2 internal handoffs?

**YES -- step-granular entries enable single-read pipeline verification; section-level entries require PART 2 traversal.**

V-02 demonstrates: with `PART 2 STEP E (by label)` in both PART 2 internal rows, a reader can confirm that STEP E is the correct consuming step for both Inertia case and Confirmed readiness without reading PART 2. The 11-row table in V-02 is a complete audit point for the PART 1-to-PART 2 handoffs and the PART 2 internal chain.

V-01 and V-03's `PART 2 readiness summary` entries require the reader to open the PART 2 section to confirm which specific step (STEP E vs. STEP B or STEP D) actually consumes those outputs -- the table does not answer the question. C-37's contribution is overhead reduction: it converts the ARCHITECTURE block from a pipeline summary that requires cross-referencing into a pipeline contract that can be audited in one read.

### RQ3: Does C-36 add structural enforcement beyond C-28/C-34 at the STEP E convergence point?

**PARTIALLY -- C-36 is the convergence-point-specific name for what C-28/C-34 jointly prohibit, and C-36 FAIL structurally entails C-28/C-34 FAIL. The three form a structural cluster at STEP E, not three independent criteria.**

V-03 PASS confirms that independent self-standing form satisfies C-36, C-34, and C-28 simultaneously. V-01/V-02's grouped-header form fails all three. There is no form that passes C-36 while failing C-28, or passes C-28 while failing C-36, at the convergence point where two named inputs meet.

What C-36 adds over C-28/C-34: precision about what "independent" means at the convergence point. C-28 prohibits grouped annotations generally. C-34 requires two separate prohibitions at STEP E specifically. C-36 specifies that each annotation must be "independently readable as a complete statement... without relying on proximity to the other prohibition for interpretation." This is a tighter semantic constraint than C-28/C-34's syntactic separate-line requirement, but at execution time the forms that satisfy the tighter constraint are the same forms that satisfy the looser ones. C-36's value is definitional: it names the property (independence) rather than adding a detectably different execution path.

**Scoring asymmetry**: V-01 and V-02 fail three criteria (C-36, C-28, C-34) through one design choice (grouped annotation) -- scoring 27/30. V-03 fails two criteria (C-37, C-38) through two design choices (section-level table, no terminal verdict) -- scoring 28/30. V-03 scores one point higher than V-01/V-02 because the C-36/C-28/C-34 cluster counts as three criterion failures from one structural failure, while V-03's two failures are from two distinct structural choices.
