## Excellence Signals — Patterns from Top-Scoring Variations

### Signal 1: Protocol Names Embedded in Heading Manifests (V-04, V-05)

V-04 and V-05 extend C-17 by including sub-protocol names directly in the section heading manifest entries. V-04: "Phase 2: Switching Cost -- Switching Cost Protocol". V-05: "A. Switching Cost Table -- Switching Cost Protocol". The result: the named sub-protocol (C-19) is pre-committed at two independent structural sites — the manifest (document level) and the section heading (execution level). Omitting or renaming the sub-protocol creates a mismatch detectable at both sites. Variations with C-19 via procedural role instructions (V-03) lack this double-declaration.

### Signal 2: Phase Boundary as Protocol Declaration (V-04)

V-04 fuses the phase header and the protocol name into a single structural element. "PHASE 3: BREAK THE WORKAROUND -- Failure Mode Standard" is both the section heading (C-14/C-17) and the protocol declaration (C-19). This eliminates a separate label step — the protocol is declared exactly once at the execution site, with no opportunity for the instruction to arrive before the requirement is written or after. The phase-as-protocol pattern compresses two structural properties into one line.

### Signal 3: Numbered Sub-Requirements as Natural C-20 Anchor Sites (observed across V-03, V-04, V-05)

Comprehensive C-19 (all multi-component requirements named with numbered sub-items) creates structural anchor sites where inline pass/fail pairs attach naturally. Each numbered sub-requirement is a specific testable assertion: "1. Tool names: product-level, not category-level." This naturally invites "Pass: X / Fail: Y" at that exact position. V-01 and V-02 with a single named protocol have 3 natural pair sites; V-03, V-04, V-05 with 5 named protocols each create 10–15 pair sites. The C-19/C-20 co-occurrence pattern is structural, not incidental.
