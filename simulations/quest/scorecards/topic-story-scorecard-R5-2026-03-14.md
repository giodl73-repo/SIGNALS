## Excellence Signals

**From V-04 and V-05 (tied top-scorers):**

**Signal 1 — Position is the C-19 mechanism, not content.** V-03 and V-04 carry nearly identical structural requirements in their checklists, but V-03's 4-item check appears *after* all sections and V-04's 12-item check appears *before*. V-03 fails C-19; V-04 passes. The rubric fires on the gate property, not the checklist's comprehensiveness. A post-template review step — even a thorough one — is architecturally equivalent to no pre-writing gate.

**Signal 2 — Verbatim strings in failure mode descriptions satisfy C-21 independently of directive format.** V-03 embeds the verbatim scan strings inside failure mode descriptions (no standalone "Anti-neutral directive" label) and passes C-21. V-05 embeds them inside a labeled "Anti-neutral directive" and also passes C-21. The discriminator for C-21 is whether named strings are *present inside the section* — not whether they appear inside a failure mode vs. a standalone directive. C-17 + verbatim strings = C-21 satisfaction regardless of delivery format.

**Signal 3 — `**This claim fails if**:` satisfies C-20.** V-01 and V-04 use `**This claim fails if**: [...]` rather than the canonical `**Disproof condition**: [...]` and both pass C-20. The labeled field structure is the mechanism — the label name is not the discriminator, provided the label is bold, colon-terminated, and followed by a placeholder. `*Fails if*: [...]` form from the rubric's examples is confirmed valid.

**Signal 4 — The three R5 criteria are compositionally independent.** V-04 adds C-19 and C-21 (not C-20, since `**This claim fails if**:` already covered it) and scores 100. V-02 adds only C-20 and misses C-19 and C-21. V-03 adds only C-21 and misses C-19 and C-20. No regression observed — the mechanisms do not interfere with each other or with inherited criteria. The criteria accumulate additively.
