## What Made It Golden

**1. Three-position rule label normalization**
All three structural rule labels -- inventory (A-16), citation (A-19), and fail-first (A-31) -- carry the full `[DOMAIN] [DESCRIPTOR] RULE [CRITERION-ID]` schema. Earlier rounds confirmed the pattern only at the A-16 position. R20 proved it holds at citation (Section 6) and fail-first (declaration body) too -- structurally distinct positions with different authoring contexts.

**2. Bracket-suffix criterion ID as terminal element (A-46)**
The criterion ID appears as the final bracketed token in every rule label. This makes each label self-documenting and cross-referenceable by inspection. The terminal position is not cosmetic -- it is the anchor the rubric uses to verify label completeness without prose interpretation.

**3. RULE as the normalized structural noun (A-47)**
No alternative noun (CONSTRAINT, LOCK, RESTRICTION, REQUIREMENT) appears at the structural noun position in any of the five variations. RULE is used exclusively, making the three-label family internally consistent and giving the criterion-ID suffix stable meaning across positions.

**4. Domain prefix coherence across all three positions (A-42)**
The axis subject (INERTIA THREAT, STATUS QUO, INCUMBENT) prefixes all three rule labels in every variation. The same inertia framing that appears in the inventory heading propagates into the citation rule and the fail-first declaration -- a single structural argument across three positions rather than three unrelated labels.

**5. Axis isolation strategy produces traceable predictions**
Single-axis variations (V-01, V-02, V-03) each change exactly one parameter against the baseline, making any failure attributable to a specific criterion. Combined variations (V-04, V-05) test multi-axis stability. Every 290/290 prediction is traceable to a specific structural claim -- not hedged confidence, but verified structural coverage.
