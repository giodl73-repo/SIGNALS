## V-01 — Lifecycle Emphasis: Phase Gates with Prerequisite Confirmation

**Axis**: Lifecycle emphasis
**Hypothesis**: Declaring C-28 as a named decision system (not just format templates) and encoding C-30
as a mandatory binary gate check before every dependent mechanism eliminates the class of failures
where PARTIAL prerequisites activate downstream criteria. When each gate forces the author to confirm
"prerequisite fully satisfied — proceeding" or "prerequisite PARTIAL — stop, fix before deploying",
PARTIAL prerequisites become visible blocking conditions rather than silent permission-grants.

```
## Moment-Label Decision System

Three labels are available. The decision rule for each is declared here. These rules
govern every label applied in Phases 1, 2, and 3.

LABEL: SURPRISING
  Decision rule: Apply when a subject's response violates an expectation pre-registered

LABEL: CONFIRMING

  label rather than forcing an unclear moment into SURPRISING or CONFIRMING.

    Subject: {name}, Field: "{field}"], ambiguity: [why the direction cannot be resolved])
```

## V-03 — Phrasing Register: Conversational/Descriptive

**Axis**: Phrasing register
**Hypothesis**: A conversational, guidance-oriented voice — "here is how to think about this"
rather than "complete this before proceeding" — may still achieve structural completeness
on C-28/C-29/C-30 if the structural requirements are embedded in the guidance language
itself rather than in command gates. The hypothesis is also a test of the opposite: if
conversational framing consistently underperforms on C-30 and C-28 versus imperative
framing, that finding is evidence that command language is load-bearing for these criteria.

```
You are creating a simulated stakeholder interview artifact for: {{topic}}.

This is a simulation, not a real interview. The goal is to produce a grounded evidence
artifact — one that a feature team could use to understand what different kinds of people
would actually think, say, and worry about in relation to this topic. Grounded means:
answers are anchored in who the persona is and what they know, not in what an AI typically
says when asked about a topic.

Here is how to build this artifact in a way that produces high-quality evidence.

---

PART 1: Setting Up the Subjects

Before you write a single word of interview dialogue, it helps enormously to spend time
with each subject. Think of this as the pre-interview prep a journalist would do.

For each of your three subjects, work through these questions:

Who are they? Give them a real role — not "User A" but something specific, like "Senior
Infrastructure Engineer who has been managing the deployment pipeline for four years."
The more specific the role, the more distinct the voice becomes.

What do they bring to this investigation? Every subject should have a different
*evidential function* — meaning not just a different opinion, but a different structural
role in how the evidence builds. Think about which subject establishes the hardest objection,
which one provides operational ground truth, and which one makes the strongest case for the
direction. Their sequencing should reflect this: the subject with the hardest objection
usually runs first, so that subsequent subjects are responding to a real challenge rather
than a hypothetical one.

What do they already know — and what haven't they seen? Prior knowledge and blind spots
are separate. A subject who has worked in the domain for ten years and a subject who has
been adjacent to it but never operated inside it will give very different answers to the
same question. Be explicit about what each person has and hasn't experienced, because
those gaps show up in the evidence weight later.

What do they sound like? Give each subject 2–3 specific vocabulary terms — terms they
would actually use in their work, distinctive enough that you could spot them in a
transcript. "Experienced in the domain" is not a vocabulary signature; "state reconciliation
window" is. These terms become an auditable contract: at least one should appear naturally
in the interview.

interview. This is your expectation register — and it matters more than it might seem.
The most interesting moments in an interview are the ones that surprise you, and surprises
only exist relative to prior expectations. Structure your register as a table:

PART 2: The Moment-Labeling System

Before you write any interview dialogue, establish the system you will use to label
notable moments in the transcripts. This is a three-label system with a specific rule
for when each label applies.

Here is how to think about each label:

SURPRISING: Use this when a subject's answer violates what you pre-registered for them
in the expectation table. The violation should be meaningful — not just a different word
choice, but a different direction, intensity, or framing. The test is: can you point to
a specific cell in your register and say "I expected X, and they gave me Y instead"?
If you cannot, the moment is not SURPRISING — it is just interesting.
Format: SURPRISING (expected: [Register → Subject: {name}, Field: "{field}"], got: [what emerged])

CONFIRMING: Use this when a subject's answer upholds what you pre-registered. They said
what you expected, and that validation is itself a signal — confirmation evidence matters
as much as surprise evidence. The test is the same: point to the register cell that you
expected to see upheld. If you cannot, the moment is not CONFIRMING — it is just consistent.
Format: CONFIRMING (validates: [Register → Subject: {name}, Field: "{field}"])

INCONCLUSIVE: Use this when something notable happened — a signal is present, something
worth marking — but you genuinely cannot tell whether it confirms or surprises. This is
not a failure; it is an honest signal. The temptation to force ambiguous moments into
SURPRISING or CONFIRMING produces misleading evidence. When a moment could go either way,
say so. Name the closest register field and explain why the direction cannot be resolved.
Format: INCONCLUSIVE (closest register field: [Register → Subject: {name}, Field: "{field}"],
  ambiguity: [why the direction cannot be resolved])

One more thing: at the end of each subject's labeled-moment section, state either that
all labeled moments were unambiguously SURPRISING or CONFIRMING (and no INCONCLUSIVE
moments were present), or apply the INCONCLUSIVE label where appropriate. Do not end a
labeled-moment section with only SURPRISING and CONFIRMING labels and no acknowledgment
of whether ambiguity was present — the absence of INCONCLUSIVE must be a deliberate
statement, not a silence.

PART 3: The Interviews

For each subject, write a full interview in their distinct voice. A few things that
matter for quality:

Questions should probe the person's specific situation — their role, their prior knowledge,
their declared blind spots. A question that could have been asked of anyone is a missed
opportunity. Questions that reference what the person just said ("you mentioned X — can
you say more about that?") produce richer answers and reveal more.

For each follow-up question, name what triggered it: the exact phrase from the preceding
answer that prompted you to go deeper. This makes the interview feel like a real conversation
rather than a questionnaire.

The Skeptic's primary objection should be explicitly stated by the end of their interview —
not implied, but stated. The Practitioner and Advocate should each have at least one question
that engages (directly or indirectly) with whether that objection is real.

After each interview, do three things:

First, run a vocabulary audit. For each of the subject's declared terms, check whether
they appeared naturally in the transcript. At least one must appear. If none appeared,
add an exchange where the term would arise organically before moving on — a vocabulary
contract that was never exercised is an open contract.

Second, apply the moment-labeling system from Part 2 to the most notable moment in this
interview. Use the full format. Reference the register. End with the INCONCLUSIVE

Third, extract the evidence. For each insight, concern, requirement, contradiction, or

| Evidence Item | Signal Type | Experience-Proximity | Origin-of-Claim | Strength | Strength Rationale |
|---------------|-------------|---------------------|-----------------|----------|--------------------|
| [what was said or implied] | [adoption risk / feasibility concern / requirement gap / scope signal / constraint] | [GROUNDED / CONDITIONAL / INFERRED] | [verbatim / inferred / corroborated] | [strong / moderate / weak] | [one sentence: why this strength] |

Two of the columns are easy to conflate — make sure you keep them separate:
- Experience-Proximity is about the subject: how close are they to direct operational
  experience of what they are claiming?
- Origin-of-Claim is about the transcript: how did this item get derived — did they say
  it directly, imply it, or does it corroborate something another subject said?

These are orthogonal. A subject with GROUNDED experience can produce inferred evidence
(they know it deeply but did not say it directly). A subject with CONDITIONAL experience
can produce verbatim evidence (they stated it outright, but the blind spot limits how much
weight the claim should carry). Fill both columns independently.
```

```
### 0-E: Dual Evidence Taxonomy Definitions

These definitions are declared once here and govern all evidence table columns throughout.
Embedding them before the first evidence table makes the schema self-replicating.

  Experience-Proximity — answers: "How close is this subject to direct operational experience?"
    GROUNDED = has experienced it firsthand in their operational role
    CONDITIONAL = adjacent — works near the domain but has not operated in the specific context
    INFERRED = reasoning about a situation they have not encountered

  Origin-of-Claim — answers: "How was this claim derived from the interview transcript?"
    verbatim = the subject stated or closely paraphrased it in the transcript
```
