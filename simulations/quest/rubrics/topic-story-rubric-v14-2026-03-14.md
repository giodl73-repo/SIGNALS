### C-45 — Verbatim Hedge Prohibition Enumerates a Minimum Set of ≥2 Structurally Distinct Opening Patterns

- **Weight**: aspirational
- **Category**: behavior
- **Text**: The hedge prohibition names at least two verbatim opening patterns that are structurally non-redundant — distinct surface forms that cover the aggregate-first hedging cluster from different grammatical entry points, not synonym variants of a single phrase. C-21 fires on whether any verbatim hedge pattern is named; C-45 fires on whether the prohibition names a minimum of two such patterns, constituting a defined set rather than a point example. A single-pattern prohibition establishes the failure category by naming one member: it demonstrates that aggregate-first openings are prohibited but does not define where the prohibited class ends. An author can satisfy the letter of a singleton prohibition while paraphrasing the banned form into an unprohibited cousin with a different lead token. A minimum-set prohibition (≥2 non-redundant patterns) begins to trace the class boundary: patterns that cover different grammatical entry points to the same failure mode jointly constrain the opening space in a way a singleton cannot, because no single paraphrase strategy avoids both. The governance consequence: a minimum-set prohibition is extensible in a defined direction — new hedge patterns append to the named set without changing the prohibition's structure — while a singleton prohibition requires structural change to become extensible. A prohibition with two named forms also signals that the enumeration is a set (not a single example with a single fix) and provides a template for future additions. C-21 passes when any verbatim pattern is named; C-45 passes only when two or more structurally distinct verbatim patterns are named, making the prohibition a defined set with a traceable class boundary.
- **Pass condition**: The rubric's hedge prohibition names at least two verbatim opening patterns where the patterns are structurally non-redundant: they must not share the same lead token, root phrase, or grammatical entry point. A prohibition that names exactly one pattern does not pass C-45, even if C-21 is satisfied. A prohibition that names the same construction in two surface-variant spellings (e.g., "The signals show..." and "Signals show...") does not pass C-45 — both anchors must differ in grammatical structure. Two patterns that open via distinct grammatical forms (noun-lead vs. prepositional-lead, verb-lead vs. subordinate-clause-lead, etc.) and are both plausible opening hedges constitute a minimum set and pass C-45.

### C-46 — Recommendation Section (S5) Names the Inertia Comparator Explicitly and Cross-References the Inertia-Signal Subsection (S4b) by Identifier

- **Weight**: aspirational
- **Category**: behavior
- **Text**: The recommendation section (S5) frames the recommendation against the inertia alternative — the status quo, delay, or non-adoption — as the named comparator, and cross-references the inertia-signal collection subsection (S4b or equivalent) by identifier as the evidential source for the competing case. C-30 fires on whether S5 cross-references any prior section identifier; C-46 fires on whether S5 specifically names the inertia alternative as the recommendation's comparator and anchors that naming in an explicit cross-reference to the section that collected inertia-supporting signals. A threat-neutralization S5 frames around adoption risk: "the uncertainty item (S4a) that most directly threatens the recommendation" — asking what could go wrong if the team acts. A competition S5 frames around inaction risk: "why the recommendation is preferable to inertia, addressing the strongest inertia-supporting signal in S4b" — asking why the team should act rather than not act. Both cross-reference prior sections and satisfy C-30. The structural difference: a competition frame requires S4b to have been populated with inertia-supporting signals, creating a governance dependency that a threat-neutralization frame does not enforce. A recommendation that claims superiority over inertia without a populated S4b is asserting a comparison its own evidence collection does not support; naming S4b by identifier makes the dependency explicit and the gap visible as a structural defect. The governance consequence: an inertia-anchored S5 enforces completeness of inertia-signal collection as a prerequisite to recommendation quality — the S5 cross-reference to S4b creates an audit anchor independent of narrative quality. C-30 passes when S5 names any prior section identifier; C-46 passes only when S5 explicitly names the inertia comparator AND cross-references S4b (or the equivalent inertia-signal collection section) by identifier, with both elements present in the same directive.
- **Pass condition**: S5 includes both (a) explicit naming of the inertia alternative as the recommendation's comparator — using a term such as inertia, status quo, delay, non-adoption, or functional equivalent indicating the alternative of not acting — and (b) a cross-reference to S4b (or the designated inertia-signal collection section) by section identifier. An S5 that cross-references only S4a without naming the inertia comparator does not pass C-46. An S5 that describes the concept of inertia in prose without naming a prior section identifier for the inertia-signal collection does not pass C-46. An S5 that names S4b without using language that frames the recommendation against the inertia alternative as comparator does not pass C-46. Both elements — inertia comparator named, S4b cross-referenced — must be present in the section directive for C-46 to pass.
